🪟What is Microsoft?

Microsoft is one of the most consequential technology companies ever built — its software runs on over a billion computers and its cloud serves virtually every major enterprise on Earth.

Explain it like I'm 10

Imagine if one company made the operating system that runs most of the world's computers (Windows), the most popular productivity software (Word, Excel, PowerPoint), a cloud computer service that thousands of businesses use to run their websites (Azure), the world's biggest professional network (LinkedIn), the most popular place for programmers to store their code (GitHub), and one of the top gaming platforms (Xbox). That's Microsoft. It's not just one product — it's a whole collection of essential technology services.

What makes Microsoft interesting is that it nearly failed in the smartphone era — it bet on Windows Phone and lost to Apple's iPhone and Google's Android. But then a new CEO called Satya Nadella arrived in 2014 and changed the whole strategy: instead of fighting to own the device you hold in your hand, Microsoft would own the cloud "brain" that everything connects to. That shift worked brilliantly, and today Microsoft is worth more than $3 trillion.

Microsoft Corporation was founded in April 1975 by Bill Gates and Paul Allen in Albuquerque, New Mexico, with a vision of "a computer on every desk and in every home" — an audacious goal in an era when computers were the size of refrigerators and cost more than cars. The company's first major breakthrough came in 1980 when IBM needed an operating system for its new personal computer. Microsoft acquired QDOS (Quick and Dirty Operating System) from Seattle Computer Products, licensed it to IBM as MS-DOS, and critically, retained the rights to licence it to other manufacturers. This decision became the foundation of Microsoft's dominance over the PC industry.

The Windows era, beginning with Windows 3.0 in 1990, transformed Microsoft into the most powerful software company in history. By coupling Windows with the Office productivity suite and leveraging IBM-compatible PC hardware standards, Microsoft established a near-monopoly in personal computing software that drew a landmark antitrust case from the U.S. Department of Justice in 1998. The case alleged that Microsoft illegally bundled Internet Explorer with Windows to crush Netscape Navigator — an early example of the platform leverage concerns that would come to define Big Tech regulation debates decades later.

The most remarkable chapter in Microsoft's history is its cloud transformation under CEO Satya Nadella, who took the helm in February 2014. Recognising that the company had missed the mobile revolution entirely, Nadella pivoted Microsoft's strategy to "cloud-first, mobile-first" — investing heavily in Azure cloud computing, making Office available on iOS and Android, open-sourcing .NET, and acquiring LinkedIn ($26B, 2016), GitHub ($7.5B, 2018), and Activision Blizzard ($69B, 2023). The results have been extraordinary: Microsoft's market capitalisation grew from approximately $300 billion in 2014 to over $3 trillion in 2024, making it briefly the world's most valuable company.

⚙️How Microsoft's Business Works

Microsoft is organised into three major segments: Productivity and Business Processes, Intelligent Cloud, and More Personal Computing — each a multi-billion-dollar business in its own right.

The Intelligent Cloud segment, centred on Microsoft Azure, is Microsoft's fastest-growing and strategically most important business. Azure is the world's second-largest cloud computing platform (after Amazon Web Services), offering over 200 cloud services including virtual machines, databases, AI/ML services, DevOps tools, and IoT platforms. Azure's revenue grew at over 30% annually for several years and now contributes approximately $110 billion annually to Microsoft's top line. Crucially, Azure is the infrastructure backbone for Microsoft's own services including Office 365, Teams, Xbox Live, LinkedIn, and Bing.

The Productivity and Business Processes segment covers Office 365 commercial and consumer subscriptions, LinkedIn, and Dynamics 365 (CRM and ERP software). LinkedIn, acquired for $26 billion in 2016, has proven to be one of Microsoft's most valuable acquisitions — it now generates over $16 billion annually through premium subscriptions, talent solutions (recruiting tools), marketing solutions, and learning (LinkedIn Learning). The platform's 1 billion members and professional data assets create significant AI training value for Microsoft's models.

The More Personal Computing segment includes Windows OEM licences (paid by PC manufacturers), Xbox hardware and Game Pass subscriptions, Surface devices, and Bing advertising. While this segment is the smallest relative contributor to revenue growth, Windows remains strategically important as the foundation of Microsoft's enterprise relationships. The $13 billion investment in OpenAI — the creator of ChatGPT — represents Microsoft's boldest AI bet, with OpenAI's models now powering Copilot across all Microsoft products.

💰How Does Microsoft Make Money?

Microsoft's revenue model spans hardware, software licences, cloud subscriptions, advertising, and gaming — one of the most diversified revenue streams in tech.

Azure cloud computing is Microsoft's primary growth engine. Businesses pay for Azure services on a consumption model — the more computing resources they use, the more they pay. This includes virtual machines, storage, databases, AI services, and networking. Azure's enterprise contracts are typically multi-year commitments worth millions of dollars annually, creating highly predictable revenue. The combination of consumption-based pricing and long-term contracts produces both growth and stability — unusual in any industry.

Microsoft 365 subscriptions are the second pillar. With 400+ million paid commercial seats and growing consumer adoption, the subscription model provides approximately $78 billion annually in highly recurring revenue. The introduction of Copilot at $30/user/month as an add-on creates a significant upsell opportunity — if even 10% of commercial users adopt Copilot, it adds $14+ billion in annual revenue from a single feature launch.

Gaming represents Microsoft's consumer entertainment bet. Xbox hardware, Xbox Game Pass subscriptions ($14.99/month for hundreds of games), and Activision Blizzard's game catalogue (Call of Duty, World of Warcraft, Candy Crush) give Microsoft a comprehensive gaming platform strategy. Game Pass had over 34 million subscribers before the Activision acquisition — a number Microsoft expects to grow significantly as Activision's top titles are added to the subscription.

📅Microsoft History

Five decades of technology history — from a BASIC interpreter for the Altair to the world's most valuable AI company.

1975 — Founded by Gates and Allen

Microsoft is incorporated in Albuquerque. First product: BASIC interpreter for the Altair 8800 microcomputer.

1981 — MS-DOS and the IBM Deal

Licenses MS-DOS to IBM for its personal computer. Retaining the right to license to others creates the foundation of Microsoft's OS monopoly.

1990 — Windows 3.0 Launches

The graphical interface makes PCs accessible to mainstream users. Windows becomes the world's dominant operating system within five years.

1995 — Internet Explorer and the Browser Wars

Internet Explorer 1.0 ships with Windows 95. Microsoft's tactics to crush Netscape lead to a landmark DOJ antitrust case.

2014 — Satya Nadella Becomes CEO

The cloud-first transformation begins. Azure investment accelerates, Office goes cross-platform, and Microsoft begins its most successful decade.

2016 — LinkedIn Acquired for $26B

Largest acquisition in Microsoft's history at the time. LinkedIn's professional data and network become a critical AI and revenue asset.

2019 — $1 Trillion Market Cap

Microsoft becomes only the third company in history to reach a $1 trillion valuation, validating the cloud transformation strategy.

2023 — OpenAI Investment and Copilot

$13B invested in OpenAI; Copilot AI launched across all Microsoft products. Microsoft establishes itself as the leading enterprise AI company.
