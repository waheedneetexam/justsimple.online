An independent deep-dive into how YouTube works, how its recommendation engine shapes what billions watch, and how it turned video into a $36 billion business.

▶️What is YouTube?

YouTube transformed the internet from a text-based medium into a video-first world — and it happened surprisingly fast.

Explain it like I'm 10

Imagine if you had a magic television that had every single video ever made by anyone in the world — cooking shows, cartoons, sports highlights, funny cat videos, science lessons, and more — and you could search for anything you wanted to watch right now, for free. That's basically YouTube. But here's the really special part: anyone can put their own videos on it, not just TV studios. Your neighbour, your teacher, or even a ten-year-old kid could upload a video and have millions of people watch it.

YouTube makes money by showing short advertisements before or during videos, a bit like regular TV commercials. The people who make the videos — called creators — get to share some of that advertising money with YouTube. So if you make great videos that lots of people watch, YouTube will pay you. That's why some YouTubers earn more money than movie stars, just from making videos in their bedroom!

YouTube is the world's largest online video platform and the second most visited website on the internet, behind only its parent company Google. It was founded in February 2005 by three former PayPal employees — Chad Hurley, Steve Chen, and Jawed Karim — who recognised that sharing video online was unnecessarily complicated and wanted to make it as simple as uploading a file. The first ever video uploaded to YouTube was an 18-second clip called "Me at the zoo," posted by co-founder Jawed Karim on April 23, 2005, showing him standing in front of elephants at the San Diego Zoo.

Google acquired YouTube in October 2006 for $1.65 billion in Google stock — a deal that initially attracted widespread scepticism given the platform's lack of a clear revenue model and its significant copyright infringement problems. That acquisition proved to be one of the most transformative business decisions in technology history. YouTube has since grown into a standalone business generating approximately $36 billion in annual revenue, dwarfing the acquisition price many times over and becoming a cultural force that has fundamentally changed entertainment, education, news consumption, and political discourse.

Today, YouTube is more than a website — it is an entire media ecosystem. It hosts content ranging from billion-view music videos and multi-season documentary series to live-streamed sporting events, real-time news coverage, university-level educational content, and billions of hours of user-generated entertainment. It operates in over 100 languages, serves 2.7 billion monthly active users, and has spawned an entirely new category of celebrity and profession: the professional YouTube content creator.

⚙️How Does YouTube Work?

From the moment a creator hits upload to the moment a viewer presses play, an extraordinary amount of engineering happens invisibly behind the scenes.

When a creator uploads a video, YouTube's infrastructure goes to work immediately. The raw video file is ingested into Google's data centres and transcoded into multiple formats and resolutions — from low-bandwidth 144p all the way to 4K and 8K — using Google's custom-built video processing pipeline. This transcoding ensures that any viewer on any device with any internet connection can stream the video smoothly. YouTube reportedly stores hundreds of exabytes of video data across distributed storage systems, making it one of the largest repositories of human-generated content in existence.

Delivery to viewers is handled through a global Content Delivery Network (CDN), which means YouTube pre-positions popular video content at servers geographically close to where viewers are located. When you press play, you're typically streaming from a server within milliseconds of your location, not from a single central server somewhere in California. YouTube also uses adaptive bitrate streaming, which continuously monitors your connection speed and automatically adjusts video quality in real-time to prevent buffering — a technology that was pioneered in part by YouTube's engineering team.

The visible part of YouTube — its homepage, search, and recommendation systems — is where the most consequential engineering decisions are made. YouTube's recommendation algorithm is responsible for approximately 70% of all video views on the platform. It analyses each viewer's watch history, search patterns, engagement signals (likes, shares, comments, and crucially, whether you finish watching or click away early), and compares these against the behaviour of millions of similar users to predict what you are most likely to watch and enjoy next. This system is extraordinarily effective at keeping viewers engaged — and has been the subject of intense criticism for its tendency to recommend increasingly extreme or emotionally provocative content.

💰How Does YouTube Make Money?

YouTube's business model is a dual-sided economy that connects advertisers to audiences and creators to revenue — with YouTube taking a substantial cut of every transaction.

YouTube's primary revenue source is advertising, which generates the vast majority of its approximately $36 billion in annual revenue. Advertisers buy ad placements through Google Ads, targeting viewers based on demographics, interests, geographic location, and the type of content being watched. Ads appear in several formats: skippable in-stream ads (which play before or during videos and can be skipped after 5 seconds), non-skippable ads (15-30 seconds that must be watched in full), bumper ads (6-second non-skippable ads), and display ads overlaid on the video or in the search results. Because YouTube knows an enormous amount about each viewer's interests and behaviour, it can charge advertisers premium rates for highly targeted placements.

YouTube shares approximately 55% of advertising revenue with the content creators who earned that revenue, retaining 45% for itself. This creator monetisation arrangement, called the YouTube Partner Program, has made it possible for thousands of creators to build full-time careers on the platform. To qualify, creators must have at least 1,000 subscribers and 4,000 watch hours in the past year. The top-earning YouTube channels — often gaming, entertainment, and children's content channels — generate millions of dollars annually from ad revenue alone.

YouTube Premium, launched in 2018 at approximately $14 per month, provides subscribers with an ad-free experience, background playback (so videos continue playing when you switch apps), offline downloads, and access to YouTube Music. While Premium represents a much smaller share of revenue than advertising, it diversifies YouTube's income and caters to the growing segment of viewers willing to pay to avoid ads. YouTube also generates revenue through Super Chats (viewer donations during live streams), channel memberships, merchandise shelves integrated into creator pages, and YouTube TV — a live television streaming service competing with traditional cable packages.

🗂️Key Features and Products

YouTube has evolved far beyond its original video-hosting roots into a multi-format entertainment and communication platform.

📱

YouTube Shorts

YouTube's answer to TikTok: vertical short-form videos up to 60 seconds. Shorts generates 70 billion daily views, making it one of the most-watched short video products on the internet. Creators earn from a dedicated Shorts ad revenue pool.

🔴

YouTube Live

Live streaming capability available to all creators above 50 subscribers. Used for gaming streams, news coverage, concerts, sports, and interactive Q&A sessions. Super Chats allow viewers to pay to have their comments highlighted during streams.

🎵

YouTube Music

A dedicated music streaming service bundled with YouTube Premium. Offers personalised playlists, offline listening, and access to millions of official tracks plus YouTube's vast collection of live recordings, covers, and remixes not available on Spotify.

📺

YouTube TV

A live TV streaming service offering 100+ cable channels for approximately $73/month. Competing directly with Hulu Live, Sling, and traditional cable, YouTube TV has attracted millions of subscribers who want live sports without a cable contract.

🔍

Search — World's 2nd Largest

YouTube is the second-largest search engine in the world by query volume, processing over 3 billion searches per month. People routinely search YouTube for tutorials, recipes, product reviews, and educational content rather than traditional web search.

💎

YouTube Premium

A subscription tier removing all advertisements, adding background playback, offline downloads, and YouTube Music access. At approximately $14/month, Premium appeals to power users who spend significant time on the platform and find ads disruptive.

📅YouTube's History and Key Milestones

YouTube's rise from a garage startup to a global media empire happened with remarkable speed — and its story is full of pivotal moments.

February 2005 — YouTube is Founded

Chad Hurley, Steve Chen, and Jawed Karim — all former PayPal employees — register the domain youtube.com after being frustrated by the difficulty of sharing video clips online. The three had been trying to share videos from a dinner party and couldn't figure out a simple way to do it. That frustration became a billion-dollar business.

April 23, 2005 — "Me at the zoo"

Co-founder Jawed Karim uploads the first YouTube video, an 18-second clip of himself standing in front of elephants at the San Diego Zoo. The video, titled "Me at the zoo," remains on YouTube to this day and has accumulated over 300 million views — a testament to the platform's extraordinary archiving of internet history.

October 2006 — Google Acquires YouTube for $1.65B

Google purchases YouTube for $1.65 billion in stock, just 18 months after the company was founded. At the time, YouTube was facing serious copyright infringement lawsuits and had no clear revenue model. The acquisition is now considered one of the greatest deals in technology history, having returned value many times the purchase price.

2009–2012 — The Creator Economy Emerges

YouTube launches the Partner Program, enabling creators to earn money from advertising revenue. This sparks the birth of the professional YouTuber. Channels like PewDiePie, Smosh, and Vsauce demonstrate that individual creators can build massive audiences without backing from traditional media companies, permanently changing the entertainment industry's power dynamics.

2017 — The "Adpocalypse"

Major advertisers pull spending after discovering their ads appearing alongside extremist and offensive content. YouTube responds with dramatic changes to its monetisation eligibility requirements and content moderation policies. The crisis accelerates YouTube's investment in automated content moderation and establishes the ongoing tension between creator freedom and advertiser safety that still defines platform policy decisions today.

2020–2021 — Pandemic Surge and Shorts Launch

COVID-19 lockdowns drive a massive surge in YouTube consumption as people turn to the platform for education, entertainment, and connection. YouTube launches YouTube Shorts in September 2020 as a direct response to TikTok's explosive growth among younger audiences, betting that its existing creator base and global distribution would give Shorts a competitive advantage over standalone short-video apps.

2023–2024 — Shorts Monetisation and AI Features

YouTube extends full Partner Program monetisation to YouTube Shorts creators, addressing a major creator complaint about lower earnings from short-form content. The platform also begins integrating AI tools for creators, including automatic dubbing into multiple languages, AI-generated chapter markers, and improved content discovery powered by large language models. Revenue surpasses $36 billion, confirming YouTube's position as one of the world's most valuable media businesses.

🏗️How YouTube's Recommendation Algorithm Works

YouTube's recommendation engine is the single most powerful editorial force on the platform — and understanding it is essential to understanding YouTube's cultural influence.

YouTube's recommendation system operates on a two-stage architecture: a candidate generation phase and a ranking phase. In the first stage, a neural network retrieves hundreds of candidate videos from YouTube's catalogue based on the viewer's watch history, search history, and demographic information, reducing billions of videos down to a manageable candidate set. In the second stage, a separate ranking neural network scores each candidate video more precisely using hundreds of additional features — including the predicted likelihood that the viewer will not just click the video but actually watch a significant portion of it.

Critically, YouTube moved away from optimising purely for click-through rates (which rewarded clickbait thumbnails) and towards optimising for watch time and viewer satisfaction (measured through post-watch surveys). This shift in objective function had profound effects: it rewarded content that genuinely engaged viewers for extended periods, which tended to favour emotionally stimulating content. Research by academics and journalists has documented that this watch-time optimisation can create "rabbit holes" where the algorithm leads viewers from moderate content toward more extreme versions of the same topics, because extreme content often generates higher emotional engagement and longer watch sessions.

🌍Impact and Why YouTube Matters

YouTube has fundamentally changed how human beings learn, entertain themselves, and communicate — with consequences both profoundly positive and deeply troubling.

YouTube democratised media production and distribution in ways that were unimaginable before 2005. Before YouTube, reaching a mass audience required access to expensive broadcast infrastructure, record label contracts, or film studio backing. YouTube eliminated these gatekeepers entirely, allowing anyone with a camera and an internet connection to reach a global audience. This has been transformative for education — the Khan Academy has taught mathematics to over 100 million learners for free on YouTube — and for underrepresented communities who found mainstream media poorly represented their lives, languages, and perspectives.

The creator economy that YouTube pioneered has become a major economic force. Estimates suggest that YouTube directly supports several hundred thousand full-time jobs for content creators in the United States alone, and many millions more worldwide when accounting for the entire ecosystem of video editors, thumbnail designers, channel managers, and sponsorship deal brokers that has grown around the platform. This represents a genuine structural shift in how creative work is organised and compensated — one that has inspired copycat creator programs at TikTok, Instagram, Twitter/X, and virtually every other major platform.

At the same time, YouTube's enormous influence over information consumption raises serious concerns. Academic researchers have documented the platform's role in amplifying conspiracy theories, political radicalisation, and health misinformation, particularly during the COVID-19 pandemic. YouTube's content moderation at scale remains an unsolved problem: with 500 hours of video uploaded every minute, human review of all content is impossible, automated systems produce significant false-positive and false-negative errors, and the line between legitimate political speech and harmful extremism is genuinely difficult to draw algorithmically.

YouTube's cultural impact on music cannot be overstated. The platform effectively replaced MTV as the primary way younger audiences discover and consume music, and its video view counts have become a major metric of musical success. The "YouTube billion views club" — videos that have exceeded one billion views — includes content from every major genre and dozens of countries, reflecting a globalisation of popular culture that the platform both reflects and drives.

✅Summary

The Bottom Line on YouTube

YouTube is the world's biggest video library and one of the most consequential media platforms ever built. It started as a simple solution to a frustrating problem — sharing videos online was too hard — and grew into a platform that now receives 500 hours of new video every single minute and serves over two billion users every month. Google bought it for $1.65 billion in 2006, which at the time seemed like a gamble; it turned out to be one of the greatest investments in business history.

The engine driving YouTube's success is not just its video hosting — it is the recommendation algorithm that determines what 70% of viewers watch next. That algorithm, optimised for watch time and engagement, keeps people watching far longer than they planned, which translates directly into more ad revenue. The platform has created an entirely new economic class of creators who earn their living from video, reshaped how music is consumed and promoted, and become one of the world's primary sources of both information and misinformation. Understanding YouTube means understanding both the extraordinary potential of open platforms and the profound challenges of governing them responsibly at planetary scale.
