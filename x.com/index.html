🤔What is X (formerly Twitter)?

A plain-language explanation of what X is and how it works — even if you've never used it.

Explained Simply

Imagine a giant public noticeboard where anyone in the world can pin a short message for everyone to see. That's X (which used to be called Twitter). Unlike private messaging apps, posts on X are public by default — when a politician announces a decision, when a disaster strikes, or when a celebrity says something controversial, it often happens on X first, before anywhere else.

You follow people whose posts you want to see, and your home feed shows a mix of posts from people you follow plus posts the algorithm thinks you'll find interesting. You can reply to anyone — that's what makes X feel like a real conversation rather than a broadcast. A short post was originally limited to 140 characters (to fit in a text message), later expanded to 280 — and now, for paying subscribers, much longer.

In October 2022, billionaire Elon Musk bought Twitter for $44 billion, fired most of its staff, renamed it X, and set about turning it into what he calls "a digital town square" and eventually an "everything app" combining messaging, payments, and AI. The result has been one of the most turbulent corporate transformations in tech history.

⚙️How X Works — Posts, Follows & the For You Algorithm

Understanding the mechanics that determine what you see on X and how your posts reach others.

At its core, X is a public microblogging platform. Users publish posts (formerly "tweets"), reply to others, repost content they like, and quote-post with added commentary. The follower model means you build a custom audience over time — but in 2022 Twitter began de-emphasising the chronological feed in favour of an algorithmic "For You" page, which shows posts from people you don't follow based on predicted interest. This shift increased engagement metrics but angered users who preferred controlling exactly what they saw.

The For You algorithm weighs multiple signals: how long you read a post, whether you reply or repost, who you follow, what topics your network engages with, and — after the Musk acquisition — whether the author subscribes to X Premium. In April 2023, Musk open-sourced portions of the recommendation algorithm, a first for a major social platform. The code revealed that X Premium subscribers receive a significant algorithmic boost, effectively meaning paying users get more visibility.

Trending topics are generated by detecting sudden spikes in specific hashtags and keywords, grouped by geography and personalised interest. They act as a real-time pulse of global conversation — and as a surface for manipulation, with coordinated campaigns regularly gaming the trending system to artificially amplify messages. X's reduced trust and safety team post-2022 has made this problem more acute.

💰How X Makes Money

X's business model is in flux — advertising still dominates, but Musk is pushing hard toward subscriptions and new revenue streams.

Advertising has historically made up around 80% of X's revenue, with brands paying for promoted posts, trending topics, and display ads. However, the Musk acquisition triggered a significant advertiser exodus. Major brands including Apple, Disney, IBM, and Comcast paused or pulled advertising in late 2022 and 2023, concerned about brand safety following content moderation rollbacks. X filed lawsuits against some advertisers and advocacy groups in 2024, alleging an illegal boycott conspiracy — a move that further complicated advertiser relations.

X Premium (formerly Twitter Blue) costs $8/month on web or $11/month on iOS and offers a blue verification checkmark, algorithmic amplification, the ability to post longer content and videos, and reduced ad frequency. The reuse of the blue checkmark — previously awarded only to verified public figures — caused widespread confusion when it became purchasable, with impersonation incidents spiking immediately after launch in November 2022. Musk views subscriptions as essential to reducing X's dependence on advertiser goodwill.

Data licensing is a third revenue stream: X charges companies and researchers for API access to its firehose of public posts. After dramatically raising API prices in 2023 — which shut out most academic researchers and third-party app developers — X reserved premium data access for a small number of enterprise clients. The integration of Grok AI (developed by Musk's xAI company) into X also creates a feedback loop where X's data trains xAI's models.

🔧Key Features

The features that define X's experience — some dating back to Twitter's founding, others added in the Musk era.

✍️

Posts (Tweets)

Short public messages up to 280 characters for free users, or much longer for X Premium subscribers. Replies, reposts, and quote posts create the conversational threads X is known for.

🎙️

Spaces — Live Audio

Live audio rooms where hosts can speak to thousands of listeners in real time. Spaces became popular for political discussions, AMA sessions, and breaking news commentary since their 2021 launch.

✅

X Premium (Blue Check)

Paid subscription giving a blue verification mark, algorithmic boost, longer posts, and fewer ads. The $8/month tier replaced the old free verification system, causing significant confusion about authenticity.

🤖

Grok AI

An AI assistant integrated directly into X, developed by Musk's xAI company. Grok can answer questions, analyse posts, and generate images — and is trained partly on X's own post data.

👥

Communities

Topic-based groups within X where only members can post and comment, offering a more focused, subreddit-like experience within the broader X ecosystem for niche interests.

📄

Long-form Articles

X Premium subscribers can publish long-form articles directly on the platform, blending microblogging with traditional publishing and reducing the need to link out to external blogs.

📅X / Twitter History Timeline

From a side project at a podcast company to the world's most controversial social media platform — the key milestones.

March 2006 — Jack Dorsey's First Tweet

Jack Dorsey sends the first tweet: "just setting up my twttr." Twitter launches publicly in July 2006, originally as an internal tool for Odeo, a podcasting startup. It quickly takes on a life of its own.

2009 — Iran Green Revolution

Twitter becomes a lifeline for Iranian protesters challenging the disputed 2009 election. The US State Department even asks Twitter to delay scheduled maintenance to keep the platform available. Real-time social media as a political tool is born.

2011 — Arab Spring

Twitter plays a central coordinating role in uprisings across Egypt, Tunisia, Libya, and Syria. Activists use it to organise protests, share footage, and communicate past government media blackouts — cementing Twitter's identity as a democracy tool.

2017 — Character Limit Doubles

Twitter doubles its character limit from 140 to 280 characters. What began as an SMS constraint had become a defining feature of Twitter's culture — the change is both celebrated and mourned.

January 2021 — Trump Suspended

Following the US Capitol storming, Twitter permanently suspends Donald Trump's account (@realDonaldTrump, 88 million followers), citing risk of incitement to further violence. The decision triggers global debate about platform power over political speech.

October 2022 — Musk Acquires Twitter for $44B

Elon Musk completes his $44 billion acquisition of Twitter. Within days he fires the CEO, CFO, and head of legal, and begins mass layoffs. He walks in carrying a sink, tweeting "let that sink in." The era of extreme disruption begins.

2023 — Rebrand to X

Musk rebrands Twitter to X, replacing the iconic blue bird logo with a white X. The URL twitter.com redirects to x.com. Many users and media organisations continue using "Twitter" despite the official rebrand.

2024 — Advertiser Lawsuits

X files lawsuits against major advertisers and the Global Alliance for Responsible Media, alleging a coordinated advertising boycott. Critics call it an attempt to use litigation to reverse the commercial consequences of Musk's content moderation decisions.

🌪️The Musk Era — Changes & Controversies

No acquisition in tech history has been as turbulent, visible, or consequential as Musk's takeover of Twitter/X.

The most immediate and dramatic change was the workforce reduction. Twitter had approximately 8,000 employees when Musk acquired it. Within six months, headcount had dropped to roughly 1,500 — a reduction of over 80%. Engineers, trust and safety specialists, content moderators, and communications staff were cut simultaneously. Musk argued the company had been massively over-staffed; critics pointed to service outages and reduced content moderation capacity as evidence the cuts went too deep.

Content moderation rollback was the most controversial policy change. Musk reinstated thousands of previously banned accounts, including those of far-right figures and conspiracy theorists, citing "free speech absolutism." He dissolved the Trust and Safety Council, an advisory group of external experts. Studies tracking hate speech on the platform showed measurable increases after the acquisition. Meanwhile, the platform's reduced safety team struggled to address coordinated harassment campaigns, misinformation during elections, and child safety violations.

The blue checkmark confusion became emblematic of the chaos. Overnight, verified badges — previously a signal of authentic identity for public figures — became something anyone could buy for $8/month. In the immediate aftermath, impersonation accounts briefly posed as Nintendo, pharma companies, and politicians, posting offensive or false content before being removed. The signal that verification once provided was permanently diluted. Despite this, X Premium has grown as a revenue source, though it remains far smaller than advertising income.

✅Summary

The X / Twitter Story in a Nutshell

Twitter was a genuine cultural invention — a global real-time public conversation layer that had no predecessor. It became the place where news broke first, where movements organised, where public figures spoke directly to the public without intermediaries. Its influence on politics, journalism, and public discourse in the 2010s was arguably unmatched by any other tech platform.

The Musk acquisition transformed it into something different and harder to define: more chaotic, more ideologically charged, financially precarious, and simultaneously expanded in some directions (longer posts, AI features, audio) while contracted in others (staff, moderation, advertiser confidence). Whether X successfully evolves into the "everything app" Musk envisions — or whether its cultural moment has passed — is the defining tech question of the mid-2020s.
