What is Facebook?

The world's largest social network — explained simply, then in depth.

👫 Imagine a giant digital town square where everyone you know hangs out...

You know how in a small town everyone knows everyone — you bump into friends at the shops, see photos of your cousin's new baby, hear about local events on the notice board? Facebook is like that, but for the entire planet. Instead of bumping into people physically, you post photos, share news, write messages, and see what everyone else is up to — all on your phone or computer.

Facebook was invented in 2004 by a university student named Mark Zuckerberg. He started it just for his college, then it spread to other colleges, then to everyone. Now over 3 billion people — nearly half the world — use Facebook every month. That's more people than live in the continents of North America, South America, and Europe combined.

Simple version: Facebook is a free app where you connect with friends and family, share photos and videos, join groups about your interests, and follow news — and it pays for itself by showing you targeted ads based on everything it knows about you.

⚙️How Does Facebook Work?

At its core, Facebook is a news feed powered by an algorithm that decides what you see.

When you open Facebook, you don't see posts in chronological order — you see what Facebook's algorithm thinks will keep you engaged longest. The algorithm weighs thousands of signals: who you interact with most, what type of content you engage with (video, photos, links), how long you pause on each post, and what your friends are reacting to. Posts that generate strong emotional reactions — especially anger and outrage — tend to spread furthest because they trigger the most comments and shares.

The business logic is straightforward: the longer you stay on Facebook, the more ads you see. Every minute you spend scrolling is a minute Facebook can monetise. This has led to legitimate criticism that the algorithm optimises for engagement at the expense of wellbeing — a dynamic documented in internal research Facebook suppressed, later revealed by whistleblower Frances Haugen in 2021.

💰How Facebook Makes Money

Facebook's product is free — which means you are the product.

Facebook earns 98% of its revenue from advertising. Advertisers pay to show targeted ads to specific groups of users — defined by age, location, interests, behaviours, income bracket, political views, relationship status, and hundreds of other data points Facebook has collected. This targeting precision is why Facebook ads are so effective: a shoe brand can show running shoe ads specifically to 28–35 year old women in London who follow fitness accounts and have recently searched for trainers.

In 2024, Meta generated $164 billion in revenue. The average revenue per user (ARPU) varies enormously: a US/Canada user generates roughly $68/year in ad revenue, while a user in Asia-Pacific generates around $6. This is why Meta works so hard to grow in developing markets — the user base is enormous even if individual ad revenue is lower.

📅Facebook's History

From college prank to global infrastructure in 20 years.

2004 — TheFacebook Launches

Mark Zuckerberg launches thefacebook.com from his Harvard dorm. Within 24 hours, 1,200 students sign up. It expands to other Ivy League universities within weeks.

2005 — Facebook.com Purchased

Zuckerberg buys the domain facebook.com for $200,000 and drops "The". Peter Thiel invests $500K for a 10% stake, valuing the company at $5M.

2006 — Opens to Everyone

Facebook removes the university email requirement and opens to anyone aged 13+. News Feed launches — users hate it initially, then can't live without it.

2008 — 100 Million Users

Sheryl Sandberg joins as COO and builds out the advertising business. Facebook overtakes MySpace as the world's largest social network.

2012 — Instagram Acquired & IPO

Facebook acquires Instagram for ~$1 billion (13 employees, 13 months old). IPO raises $16 billion at $38/share — largest tech IPO at the time.

2014 — WhatsApp & Oculus

Facebook acquires WhatsApp for $19 billion and Oculus VR for $2 billion — signalling the pivot toward messaging and virtual reality.

2018 — Cambridge Analytica Scandal

Revelations that data of 87 million users was harvested without consent and used in political targeting. Zuckerberg testifies before US Congress. Largest privacy scandal in social media history.

2021 — Rebrands to Meta

Facebook rebrands as Meta Platforms, signalling a pivot to the "metaverse." The name change is widely seen as an attempt to distance the company from its scandals.

2023–2024 — AI Pivot & Recovery

After a brutal 2022 (stock fell 65%), Meta rebounds through cost-cutting ("Year of Efficiency") and AI investment. Revenue reaches $164B in 2024.

⚖️Controversies & Impact

No company has been more central to debates about technology, democracy, and mental health.

The Cambridge Analytica scandal of 2018 was a watershed moment: it demonstrated that Facebook's data collection practices, while legal, could be weaponised for political manipulation. The data of 87 million users was harvested through a personality quiz app and used to build psychological profiles for targeted political advertising — most famously during the 2016 US presidential election and the Brexit referendum. The fallout led to billions in regulatory fines and a congressional hearing where Zuckerberg famously failed to explain basic concepts about Facebook's business model to ageing senators.

Frances Haugen's 2021 whistleblower disclosures revealed that Facebook's own internal research showed Instagram caused body image issues in teenage girls, that the algorithm amplified outrage and misinformation, and that the company consistently chose engagement metrics over user wellbeing. These revelations led to Senate hearings, EU investigations, and accelerated calls for social media regulation.

Despite the controversies, Facebook's core utility — connecting people with friends, family, and communities — remains genuinely valuable. In developing countries particularly, Facebook is often synonymous with the internet itself (the Free Basics programme provides Facebook access without data charges). For billions of people, it's how they stay in touch with family abroad, find jobs, organise community events, and access news.

✨Summary

🎯 The one-sentence version

Facebook is a free social network that connects 3 billion people and pays for itself by knowing everything about you and selling that knowledge to advertisers.

It's the world's most powerful attention machine — and understanding how it works is essential for anyone who uses it.
