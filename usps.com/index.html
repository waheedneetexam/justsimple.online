USPS: How America's Postal Service Works

An independent deep-dive into USPS's universal service obligation, the shift from letter mail to package delivery, the Delivering for America modernisation plan, and why USPS is both a government agency and a competitive $81B logistics business.

🌐 usps.com

Editorial Independence: JustSimple.Online is not affiliated with, endorsed by, or sponsored by USPS. All analysis is independent research for informational purposes only.

📬What is USPS?

USPS is America's postal service — a government agency that is also a $81B business delivering to every single address in the country, six days a week, regardless of profitability.

Explain it like I'm 10

The United States Postal Service (USPS) is the government organisation that delivers letters and packages to every house, apartment, and business in America. Every weekday (and Saturday too), a USPS mail carrier delivers to your address whether you live in New York City or a remote farm in Montana. Private companies like FedEx and UPS do not do that — they only deliver to addresses that make business sense. USPS is required by law to deliver everywhere.

USPS makes money by charging for stamps (letters) and shipping (packages). When you buy something from Amazon, USPS often delivers the package for Amazon at a cost much lower than FedEx or UPS. Because USPS has to deliver everywhere anyway, it can offer Amazon a good rate to add packages to its existing mail routes. People also use USPS to ship packages themselves — Priority Mail gets things there in 1-3 days, and Priority Mail Express guarantees next-day delivery.

The United States Postal Service was established in 1971 as an independent agency of the US federal government, replacing the older Post Office Department (which dates to 1775 and Benjamin Franklin as the first Postmaster General). USPS operates under a unique legal mandate: the Universal Service Obligation (USO) requires USPS to provide postal services to every address in the United States at uniform prices — a letter costs the same stamp to send from Manhattan to rural Alaska. No private carrier is obligated to offer this universal service.

USPS operates as a self-funding agency — it receives no taxpayer appropriations for operating expenses and must generate revenue to cover its costs. The tension between its public service mandate (serve all addresses at uniform prices) and its commercial requirement (financial self-sufficiency) is a persistent structural challenge. Letter mail volumes have declined over 50% since 2000 as email replaced physical correspondence. Package delivery has grown significantly as e-commerce expanded, though parcel delivery has different economics than letter mail.

The Delivering for America plan, announced in 2021, is a 10-year $40B investment to modernise USPS. The plan includes a new fleet of 66,000 Next Generation Delivery Vehicles (NGDVs) including electric vehicles, new processing facilities, and operational reforms. The plan targets long-term financial sustainability through package delivery growth, service improvements, and cost reduction.

⚙️How USPS Works

USPS operates as a universal delivery network using a network of processing facilities, transportation routes, and carrier routes to reach every US address six days per week.

USPS processes mail and packages through a network of approximately 270 processing and distribution facilities across the US. Letters and flat mail are sorted by optical character recognition machines that read addresses and apply barcodes, enabling automated sortation to carrier routes. Packages use barcode scanning at each transfer point for tracking. The network uses dedicated USPS trucks (230,000 vehicles), contracted air carriers, and contracted surface transportation for cross-country movement of mail and packages.

Carrier routes are USPS's last-mile delivery network. Each carrier route covers a specific geographic area — typically 500-600 addresses for walking routes (urban) or up to 1,000+ for driving routes (suburban/rural). Letter carriers walk or drive their routes daily, delivering whatever is assigned to that address. This universal last-mile coverage is USPS's structural advantage for e-commerce companies: USPS can add a package to an existing route at a marginal cost much lower than FedEx or UPS.

USPS.com provides digital services including package tracking, Rate Calculator, Click-N-Ship (online label creation with discounted rates), USPS Informed Delivery (email preview of incoming mail and packages), Hold Mail, Change of Address, PO Box management, and passport application scheduling. USPS APIs enable e-commerce platforms and shipping software to integrate USPS rates and tracking.

💰How USPS Makes Money

USPS generates revenue from package delivery (now the majority), stamps and letter mail, marketing mail (advertising), and services like passport and PO Box rental.

Package delivery is now USPS's largest revenue source, surpassing letter mail revenue. Amazon (USPS's largest package customer) ships millions of packages daily through USPS, using USPS last-mile delivery for residential addresses. Priority Mail, Priority Mail Express, and Parcel Select generate the majority of package revenue. Flat-rate products (Flat Rate boxes) are particularly popular with small businesses and online sellers for predictable pricing.

Stamps and First-Class Mail remain significant revenue sources despite declining volumes. The First-Class Mail price is regulated — USPS cannot set prices freely but must apply through the Postal Regulatory Commission. First-Class Mail volume has declined from 100B pieces/year in 2000 to under 50B/year as email and electronic billing replaced physical correspondence.

Marketing mail (advertising circulars, catalogs, and promotional mail) is sent at lower per-piece rates in bulk, representing approximately 30% of USPS revenue. Services revenue — money orders, passport acceptance, PO Box rentals, and special services — contributes supplemental income across USPS's retail network of 31,000 Post Office locations.

✨What Makes USPS Unique

USPS's universal service mandate, scale of physical infrastructure, and last-mile reach create a delivery network that private carriers cannot fully replicate.

🌎

Universal Service Obligation

Required by law to deliver to every US address at uniform rates. No private carrier does this — FedEx and UPS don't serve every rural address or charge the same regardless of distance.

📦

Amazon Partnership

USPS delivers a large share of Amazon's residential packages, particularly the final mile for suburban and rural addresses where existing USPS routes provide cost advantages over FedEx or UPS.

💰

Flat Rate Shipping

Flat Rate boxes with one price regardless of weight (up to 70 lbs) are uniquely popular with small businesses and online sellers shipping heavy items affordably.

📱

Informed Delivery

Free email preview of incoming mail and package tracking notifications. A unique USPS service that pre-notifies recipients before delivery — popular with households monitoring important mail.

🚚

World's Largest Civilian Fleet

230,000 vehicles operating the most extensive civilian delivery fleet in the world. New electric vehicles being added through the Delivering for America plan.

🏛️

Government Trust

As a government agency, USPS carries institutional trust for sensitive documents, legal notices, medication delivery (VA prescriptions), and voting by mail.

📅History & Timeline

From Benjamin Franklin's post office to America's modern last-mile delivery network.

1775 — Benjamin Franklin Named First Postmaster General

The Continental Congress establishes the US postal system with Benjamin Franklin as Postmaster General, predating the Constitution itself.

1863 — Free City Delivery Launches

USPS introduces free home delivery in cities, replacing the model where recipients had to collect mail from post offices.

1971 — USPS Reorganised as Independent Agency

Post Office Department becomes the United States Postal Service, an independent establishment required to be financially self-sufficient.

2006 — Postal Accountability Act

Legislation requires USPS to pre-fund retiree health benefits 75 years in advance — an obligation cited as a primary source of USPS financial losses.

2021 — Delivering for America Plan

USPS announces $40B 10-year modernisation plan including electric vehicles, new sorting equipment, and operational reforms.

2024 — Electric Vehicle Fleet Transition

USPS begins deploying electric Next Generation Delivery Vehicles across the US fleet.
