Samsung: How the World's Largest Tech Manufacturer Works

An independent deep-dive into Samsung Electronics' semiconductor empire, smartphone business, vertical integration strategy, and its unique position as the world's biggest supplier to its own biggest competitor.

🌐 samsung.com

📱What is Samsung?

Samsung is the technology company that simultaneously makes the world's most popular Android phones AND the chips inside many iPhones — a business structure with no parallel in the industry.

Explain it like I'm 10

Samsung is a huge South Korean company that makes all sorts of electronics — smartphones, TVs, refrigerators, washing machines, and also the tiny computer chips inside almost every electronic device in the world. You might own a Samsung phone, or a Samsung TV, or have a Samsung chip in your computer without even knowing it.

The really interesting thing about Samsung is that it makes parts for other companies' products, including Apple. Samsung's screens are in many iPhones, and Samsung makes memory chips that go into computers and phones from dozens of companies. So Samsung competes with Apple in selling phones, but also sells Apple the parts Apple needs to build those phones. This makes Samsung one of the most unusual companies in the world.

Samsung Electronics Co., Ltd. is a subsidiary of the Samsung Group (a South Korean chaebol — family-controlled conglomerate), and the world's largest technology company by revenue. Founded in 1969 as a subsidiary of Lee Byung-chul's Samsung Group (which began as a trading company in 1938), Samsung Electronics grew through South Korea's state-directed industrialisation to become a global leader in consumer electronics. The company has been controlled by the Lee family across three generations, with Lee Jae-yong (Jay Y. Lee) serving as executive chairman following his father Lee Kun-hee's death in 2020.

Samsung's business is structured across three main segments: Device Solutions (semiconductors and displays), MX (Mobile eXperience — smartphones and PCs), and VD/DA (Visual Display and Digital Appliances — TVs, monitors, refrigerators, washing machines). The semiconductor division makes Samsung unique among consumer electronics companies: it is simultaneously the world's largest DRAM memory chip manufacturer, the world's largest NAND flash storage manufacturer, and the world's second-largest foundry (contract chip manufacturer) behind TSMC.

Samsung's vertical integration is the defining feature of its competitive position. Unlike Apple, which designs chips but outsources manufacturing, or Qualcomm, which designs chips and outsources manufacturing, Samsung both designs and manufactures semiconductors, OLED displays, camera sensors, and batteries. This means Samsung can supply itself with components at cost while selling the same components to competitors — a structural advantage that no Western competitor can easily replicate due to the enormous capital investment required.

⚙️How Samsung Works

Samsung's operations span chip fabs, display factories, phone assembly lines, and software — all integrated under one corporate structure.

Samsung's semiconductor manufacturing (Device Solutions) operates multiple fabrication plants (fabs) in South Korea (Hwaseong, Pyeongtaek, Giheung) and Texas (Taylor, US). These fabs produce DRAM, NAND flash, and logic chips on Samsung's own process nodes. Samsung's advanced node development (3nm and 2nm GAA — Gate-All-Around) is the primary competitive arena with TSMC for logic chip manufacturing. Memory chips (DRAM and NAND) are commoditised products where Samsung competes on cost, yield, and performance against SK Hynix and Micron.

Samsung Display produces OLED panels for Samsung's own Galaxy phones and sells panels to Apple (iPhone OLED), other smartphone makers, and TV manufacturers. The display business generates significant B2B revenue and gives Samsung proprietary access to the best display technology before competitors. Samsung's camera sensor business (ISOCELL) similarly supplies both Samsung phones and third-party manufacturers with competitive imaging technology.

Samsung's mobile software stack — One UI on Android — is one of the most customised Android implementations. Samsung adds features including Samsung DeX (desktop mode), Samsung Pay, Samsung Health, Bixby (AI assistant), and deep integration with Samsung's own hardware ecosystem (Galaxy Watch, Galaxy Buds, Galaxy tablets). The software investment is necessary to differentiate Galaxy phones from other Android devices, but Samsung lacks the app ecosystem control that gives Apple strategic pricing power.

💰How Samsung Makes Money

Samsung's revenue is split roughly equally between its B2B semiconductor/display business and its B2C consumer electronics business.

Semiconductors (DS Division) generate roughly 30-40% of Samsung's revenue but are highly cyclical. In boom years, DRAM and NAND prices are high and semiconductor profits subsidise the rest of the business. In down cycles (2022-2023 saw a severe memory downturn), the semiconductor division can post operating losses. Samsung's advanced foundry business (manufacturing chips for Apple, Qualcomm, and others) is growing as a strategic priority, though TSMC maintains a significant technological lead in the most advanced nodes.

Mobile (MX Division) is Samsung's largest revenue segment by volume. The Galaxy S series (flagship), Galaxy A series (mid-range), and Galaxy Z series (foldables) span every price tier. Samsung's foldable phone strategy — Galaxy Z Fold and Z Flip — positions it as the pioneer in foldable form factors, with no direct Apple competition yet. Samsung's mobile business is profitable but under constant pressure from Apple at the premium end and Chinese manufacturers (Xiaomi, OPPO, Vivo) at the mid-range.

Samsung's display business (OLED panels) is a strategic goldmine: Samsung charges Apple a premium for iPhone OLED panels while using the same technology in Galaxy phones at internal transfer pricing. This asymmetry — supplying a competitor at premium prices — generates significant profit. Samsung's TV business (QLED and MicroLED) generates lower margins but maintains brand presence in the consumer living room, complemented by Samsung's Tizen smart TV platform which generates advertising and app revenue.

✨What Makes Samsung Unique

Samsung's combination of manufacturing scale, vertical integration, and diversification creates competitive advantages no pure-play consumer electronics company can match.

🏭

Semiconductor Manufacturing

Runs some of the world's most advanced chip fabs. Samsung is one of only two companies (with TSMC) capable of manufacturing at 3nm — supplying the entire tech industry.

🔗

Vertical Integration

Makes the chips, screens, cameras, and batteries in its own products AND sells them to competitors. No other company spans both B2B components and B2C devices at this scale.

📱

Foldable Phones

Invented and leads the foldable smartphone category with Galaxy Z Fold and Z Flip. No Apple, Google, or Chinese competitor has matched Samsung's foldable volume.

📺

TV Dominance

#1 global TV brand for 18+ consecutive years. QLED and MicroLED technology keep Samsung at the premium end of the TV market.

⚡

Galaxy Ecosystem

Galaxy Watch, Galaxy Buds, Galaxy tablets, and Galaxy phones work together with deeper integration than any Android ecosystem outside Pixel.

🌏

Chaebol Scale

Samsung Group (the parent conglomerate) includes shipbuilding, construction, insurance, and hotels — giving Samsung Electronics resources and resilience unavailable to pure-tech rivals.

📅History & Timeline

From a Korean trading company to the world's largest technology manufacturer.

1938 — Samsung Group Founded

Lee Byung-chul founds Samsung as a trading company in Suwon, Korea, selling dried fish and noodles.

1969 — Samsung Electronics Founded

Electronics subsidiary established, initially making black-and-white televisions for the domestic Korean market.

1983 — Enters DRAM Memory

Samsung enters semiconductor memory manufacturing, beginning its path to becoming the world's largest memory chip producer.

1993 — Lee Kun-hee's Reform

Chairman Lee Kun-hee launches the 'New Management' initiative — 'Change everything except your wife and children.' Quality-first transformation begins.

2010 — Galaxy S Launches

Galaxy S smartphone series launches, beginning Samsung's rise to the top of the Android market and direct iPhone competition.

2019 — Galaxy Fold Launches

World's first mass-produced foldable smartphone. Creates a new premium category Samsung would dominate for years.

2023 — AI Semiconductor Push

HBM memory for AI accelerators becomes a growth driver. Samsung competes with SK Hynix to supply Nvidia's AI chips.
