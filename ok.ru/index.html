OK.ru: How Russia's Classmates Network Works

An independent deep-dive into Odnoklassniki's social networking model, its distinct audience from VKontakte, the VK Group ownership structure, and how virtual gifts and gaming drive monetisation beyond advertising.

🌐 ok.ru

Editorial Independence: JustSimple.Online is not affiliated with, endorsed by, or sponsored by Odnoklassniki (OK.ru). All analysis is independent research for informational purposes only.

👥What is OK.ru (Odnoklassniki)?

OK.ru (Odnoklassniki) is Russia's version of Classmates.com — a social network specifically built for reconnecting with school and university classmates, now serving 70M monthly users across Russia and CIS.

Explain it like I'm 10

OK.ru is a social network in Russia that means 'Classmates' in Russian. It was designed so that people could find and reconnect with friends from their school years. You can search for the name of your school, find classmates who also use OK.ru, add them as friends, and see what they have been up to. Think of it like a Russian version of Facebook, but focused on reconnecting with people from your past.

OK.ru is particularly popular with older people in Russia (aged 35-55) who grew up before the internet. One unusual feature is virtual gifts — you can buy animated or interactive gifts with real money and send them to friends' profiles as a gesture of appreciation. This virtual gift economy is a surprisingly significant source of revenue for OK.ru, as gift-sending is culturally embedded in Russian social internet usage.

Odnoklassniki was founded in March 2006 by Albert Popkov, launching just as social networking was beginning to transform global internet usage. The platform's name translates directly to 'Classmates' — reflecting its explicit positioning as a tool for reconnecting with former school and university peers. This specific use case resonated strongly in post-Soviet Russia where many people had lost contact with childhood friends through geographic dispersion, economic migration, and the social disruption of the 1990s.

OK.ru is owned by VK Group (formerly Mail.ru Group, Russia's largest internet holding company). VK Group also owns VKontakte (VK) — Russia's largest social network. The two platforms are intentionally positioned to serve distinct audiences: VK targets 18-35 year olds with content sharing, music, and messaging similar to Facebook; OK.ru targets 35-55 year olds with the classmate reconnection focus and features like virtual gifts that resonate with older demographics.

OK.ru expanded its feature set significantly from its classmate-reconnection roots to compete with VK across social categories. The platform added streaming video (including live video), music streaming, games (particularly casual mobile games popular with older demographics), and communities. However, OK.ru's core identity remains the classmate network — its onboarding still prompts new users to find their school and graduation year.

⚙️How OK.ru Works

OK.ru's social graph is built around school connections, with engagement driven by social feed content, games, music, live video, and the virtual gift economy.

OK.ru's social graph centres on education history. Users enter their schools, universities, and graduation years, enabling the platform to suggest classmate connections. Unlike Facebook's broader friend model, OK.ru's connection suggestions are specifically framed around 'people from your school' — creating a discovery mechanism that Facebook cannot replicate without the same school-year data structure. This education-anchored social graph is hard to replicate, providing OK.ru a defensible discovery advantage for older users.

Virtual gifts are OK.ru's most distinctive feature and a significant revenue source. Users can purchase animated, 3D, or interactive gifts using OK.ru's virtual currency (OK) and send them to friends' profiles. Gift-sending is a social gesture — a way to acknowledge birthdays, anniversaries, or simply express appreciation. The gift economy has cultural significance in Russian social internet, where expressing emotions through digital gifts has lower friction than in some Western contexts.

OK.ru's games platform focuses on casual games popular with the older demographic — puzzles, card games, word games, and social farming games. The games are integrated directly into the social feed, allowing players to share scores, challenge friends, and compete on leaderboards. This social gaming approach drives engagement and in-app purchases within games, generating gaming revenue on top of advertising and virtual gift income.

💰How OK.ru Makes Money

OK.ru monetises through advertising, virtual gift purchases, gaming revenue, and premium features.

Advertising across OK.ru's social feed, video content, and games is the primary revenue driver. OK.ru's 70M monthly users in Russia and CIS are valuable for Russian-language digital advertising — brands targeting Russian consumers pay to reach OK.ru's older, often higher-income demographic. Video advertising within OK.ru's extensive video library commands higher CPMs than standard display.

Virtual gifts generate significant direct revenue from users purchasing OK virtual currency to send gifts. The gift economy is culturally embedded in OK.ru's user base — birthdays, holidays, and personal milestones are marked with virtual gift sending. Gift revenue is high-margin (virtual goods have near-zero marginal cost) and generates predictable recurring revenue from engaged social users.

Gaming revenue from in-app purchases in OK.ru's casual games contributes a third revenue stream. Players purchase in-game items, lives, power-ups, and decorations using real money or OK virtual currency. The social gaming model where friends can see each other's progress creates social proof for spending. VK Group reports social gaming as a meaningful revenue segment across its social platform portfolio.

✨What Makes OK.ru Unique

OK.ru's classmate social graph, virtual gift economy, and older demographic positioning create a distinct platform identity within Russia's social media landscape.

🎓

Classmate Discovery

School-anchored social graph that connects former classmates. Cannot be replicated by Facebook or VK without the same school-year data structure as the foundational social graph.

🎁

Virtual Gift Economy

Animated and interactive virtual gifts purchased and sent between users as social gestures. A culturally embedded feature with no direct equivalent in Western social networks.

👴

Older Demographic

Primary audience of 35-55 year olds in Russia and CIS. While younger users prefer VK or TikTok, OK.ru's older demographic often has higher disposable income for virtual gifts and games.

🌍

CIS Reach

Used across Russia, Kazakhstan, Belarus, Armenia, Azerbaijan, and other former Soviet states. The shared cultural and language heritage drives cross-border usage.

🎮

Casual Gaming

Puzzle, card, and social farming games popular with older demographics. Social leaderboards and friend challenges create game engagement loops within the social network.

🏢

VK Group Portfolio

Owned by VK Group alongside VKontakte and other internet properties. VK Group's portfolio approach covers both young (VK) and older (OK.ru) Russian demographics simultaneously.

📅History & Timeline

From a classmate reconnection tool to Russia's second social network.

2006 — Odnoklassniki Launches

Albert Popkov launches Odnoklassniki.ru in March 2006, specifically for reconnecting Russian school and university classmates.

2008 — Virtual Gifts Launch

OK.ru introduces virtual gifts purchasable with real money — the feature becomes a cultural staple and a significant revenue source.

2010 — Acquired by Mail.ru Group

Mail.ru Group (now VK Group) acquires Odnoklassniki, adding it to a Russian internet portfolio alongside VKontakte.

2014 — OK.ru Rebrand and Mobile Focus

Rebrands to ok.ru domain and launches redesigned mobile apps, adapting to smartphone-first internet usage patterns.

2018 — Live Video Streaming Launch

OK.ru adds live streaming capabilities, competing with VK Live and YouTube for Russian live video audiences.

2022 — Post-Sanctions Growth

Following geopolitical events, OK.ru continues operating as Western platforms face Russian restrictions, increasing importance as a domestic social platform.
