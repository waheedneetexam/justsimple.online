Rakuten: How Japan's Internet Ecosystem Giant Works

An independent deep-dive into Rakuten Ichiba's marketplace model, the Super Points loyalty currency that ties together 70+ services, and how Hiroshi Mikitani's vision of an online department store became one of Asia's most powerful consumer platforms.

🛒What is Rakuten?

Rakuten is Japan's version of Amazon — but with a crucial difference: it built a banking and financial services empire alongside retail, using loyalty points as the glue that makes switching away from Rakuten genuinely painful.

Explain it like I'm 10

Rakuten is like if Amazon, a bank, a mobile phone company, and Spotify were all owned by the same company and you earned the same reward points for using any of them. When you shop on Rakuten's Japanese shopping site, you earn Rakuten Points. You can spend those points when you travel with Rakuten Travel, on your phone bill (Rakuten Mobile), or when buying books (Rakuten Books). Because your points work everywhere in the Rakuten ecosystem, switching to a competitor means leaving your points behind.

Rakuten was founded in Japan in 1997 by Hiroshi Mikitani. He started it as an online shopping mall where small Japanese shops could sell to people across Japan. Now it's one of Japan's most valuable companies, with over 100 million members. Rakuten also owns Viber (a messaging app used in many countries), Kobo (an e-reader competing with Kindle), and Rakuten Rewards (a cashback site in the USA).

Rakuten Ichiba (meaning 'marketplace' in Japanese) was founded in 1997 by Hiroshi Mikitani, a Harvard MBA graduate who believed the internet could transform Japanese retail. Unlike Amazon's direct retail model, Mikitani chose a pure marketplace approach: Japanese merchants open virtual shops within the Rakuten mall, setting their own prices, branding, and customer service. This marketplace approach respected Japan's retail tradition while giving merchants national and international reach.

Rakuten Super Points is the loyalty programme that binds the ecosystem. Points are earned across all Rakuten services (typically 1% base rate on purchases) and can be spent across the same services — shopping, travel, mobile, banking, entertainment. Point multipliers for Rakuten card holders and during special campaigns can increase earn rates dramatically. This loyalty currency creates switching costs: a Rakuten Super Points balance of tens of thousands of points is a substantial financial incentive to remain within the Rakuten ecosystem.

Rakuten's global expansion strategy focused on acquisitions: Viber (global messaging), Kobo (e-readers), Ebates/Rakuten Rewards (US cashback), PriceMinister (French e-commerce), and strategic investments in Pinterest and Lyft. This acquisition-driven globalisation gave Rakuten presence across 30+ countries. The Viber acquisition particularly extended Rakuten's reach into Eastern Europe, Southeast Asia, and the Middle East.

⚙️How Rakuten Works

Rakuten operates as an integrated ecosystem where loyalty points, financial services, and content create reinforcing loops that deepen member engagement.

Rakuten Ichiba's marketplace model charges merchants a monthly fee plus commission (2% to 7% depending on category) for selling within the Rakuten mall. Unlike Amazon Japan which operates both as a marketplace and direct retailer, Rakuten Ichiba is purely a marketplace — Rakuten does not directly compete with its merchant tenants. This non-compete commitment attracts Japanese small businesses that fear Amazon's practice of competing with marketplace sellers.

Rakuten Bank operates as a full internet bank with 14M+ accounts, offering savings, loans, mortgages, and foreign exchange. Rakuten Card (credit card) has 29M+ cardholders — the fourth largest in Japan. Rakuten Securities is a leading online broker. These financial services generate significant revenue independently while providing services that Rakuten members use through the same app ecosystem.

Rakuten Mobile launched as Japan's fourth mobile carrier in April 2020, using a fully virtualised cloud-native network architecture rather than traditional physical infrastructure. Rakuten Mobile offers unlimited data plans at significantly lower prices than NTT Docomo, KDDI, and SoftBank. Despite losses in the mobile division, the strategic objective is loyalty anchor: Rakuten Mobile subscribers earn 4x points on all Rakuten purchases.

💰How Rakuten Makes Money

Rakuten's revenue combines marketplace commissions, financial services, mobile, and global service fees across 70+ business units.

Marketplace and advertising revenue from Rakuten Ichiba includes merchant commissions (2-7%), monthly store fees, and advertising products (sponsored placements, banner advertising). Japan's established retail community pays significant fees to access Rakuten's 100M member base. Advertising revenue from Rakuten's high-intent e-commerce platform commands premium CPMs relative to general display advertising.

Financial services (Rakuten Card, Rakuten Bank, Rakuten Securities) generate revenue through interchange fees on credit card transactions, net interest margin on banking assets, and trading commissions. Rakuten Card's 29M cardholders generate billions in interchange revenue annually. Financial services represent an increasingly large share of Rakuten's total revenue.

Rakuten Mobile generates subscription revenue from mobile carrier operations, though the division has operated at a loss as Rakuten builds network coverage. Rakuten Music (subscription streaming) and Rakuten Plus (the bundled subscription) generate recurring subscription revenue that diversifies away from advertising. Global services (Viber advertising, Kobo e-book sales, Rakuten Rewards cashback) contribute additional revenue streams.

✨What Makes Rakuten Unique

Rakuten's loyalty-driven ecosystem model and financial services integration create switching costs that pure e-commerce competitors cannot easily replicate.

🏅

Super Points Lock-in

Points earned across 70+ services and redeemable across the same — a loyalty currency that makes leaving Rakuten costly. Active users accumulate substantial point balances.

💳

Financial Services Moat

Credit card, banking, securities, and insurance under one membership. Financial integration deepens the relationship beyond shopping into core financial life.

📱

Rakuten Mobile Disruption

Japan's fourth mobile carrier using fully virtualised cloud network. Significantly cheaper than NTT Docomo and SoftBank while earning 4x loyalty points.

🛒

Merchant-First Marketplace

Unlike Amazon which directly competes with merchants, Rakuten Ichiba is a pure marketplace. Japanese small businesses trust Rakuten not to undercut them with private label products.

🌍

Global Acquisitions

Viber (messaging), Kobo (e-readers), Rakuten Rewards/Ebates (US cashback) extend the Rakuten brand globally. Investing in Pinterest and Lyft provided strategic fintech stakes.

🇯🇵

Japanese Cultural Fit

Rakuten's virtual shopping mall model respects Japan's retail culture of distinct shop identities within department stores — translated to digital by Mikitani's vision.

📅History & Timeline

From a 1997 online shopping mall to Japan's most powerful consumer ecosystem.

1997 — Rakuten Ichiba Launches

Hiroshi Mikitani launches Rakuten Ichiba with 13 merchants and 6 employees. The pure marketplace model attracts Japanese small businesses without Amazon-style competitive concerns.

2003 — Rakuten Bank and Card Launch

Rakuten expands into financial services with internet banking and credit card — the foundation of the Super Points ecosystem lock-in model.

2014 — Viber Acquisition

Rakuten acquires Viber for $900M, gaining 400M+ users in messaging for global reach beyond Japan.

2019 — Rakuten Mobile Launch

Rakuten launches as Japan's fourth mobile carrier with a disruptive unlimited data plan and fully virtualised network infrastructure.

2020 — US Rebrand to Rakuten Rewards

Ebates rebrands as Rakuten Rewards in the US, integrating with the global Super Points ecosystem.

2022 — Rakuten Plus Global Expansion

Rakuten expands its bundled subscription model internationally, offering music, video, cloud storage, and shopping discounts across markets.
