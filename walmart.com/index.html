Walmart: How the World's Largest Retailer Works

An independent deep-dive into Walmart's supply chain mastery, Everyday Low Price strategy, Walmart+ subscription, and how the world's largest retailer is competing with Amazon in e-commerce.

🏪What is Walmart?

Walmart is the world's largest company by revenue — the retailer that built its empire on 'Everyday Low Prices' and now serves 240 million customers per week globally.

Explain it like I'm 10

Walmart is a giant chain of stores in America (and many other countries) where you can buy food, clothes, electronics, medicines, toys, and almost everything else, all in one place. Walmart is famous for having very low prices — that's their main selling point. They can keep prices low because they buy so much from so many suppliers that they have enormous bargaining power.

Walmart also has a website and app where you can order things online and have them delivered to your home or pick them up in the store. They're working hard to compete with Amazon. Walmart employs 2.1 million people in America alone — that makes them the largest private employer in the US.

Walmart was founded in 1962 by Sam Walton in Rogers, Arkansas. Walton's insight was that rural American towns were underserved by retail — large retailers focused on urban markets, leaving small-town consumers to pay higher prices at local stores. By locating in small towns with lower real estate costs, operating with extreme efficiency, and using purchasing scale to negotiate the lowest supplier prices, Walton built a retail machine that offered prices no competitor could match.

Walmart's supply chain revolution in the 1990s established practices that the entire retail industry subsequently adopted. The installation of a private satellite network linking stores to headquarters (1987), mandatory vendor electronic data interchange (EDI), the RFID inventory tracking mandate, and the famous cross-docking distribution system (where goods arrive at a distribution centre and are immediately transferred to outbound trucks without storage) established Walmart as the technology leader in physical retail.

Walmart's e-commerce journey began slowly but has accelerated dramatically through acquisitions and investment. The $3.3B acquisition of Jet.com (and its CEO Marc Lore) in 2016 brought digital-native leadership. The Flipkart acquisition ($16B, 2018) positioned Walmart in India's massive market. Walmart+ subscription service (launched 2020) bundles free delivery, fuel discounts, and Paramount+ streaming — a direct Amazon Prime competitor. Walmart.com is now the second-largest e-commerce platform in the US.

⚙️How Walmart Works

Walmart's operational excellence — particularly its supply chain — is the foundation of its Everyday Low Price promise.

Walmart's supply chain is built around a hub-and-spoke distribution network with 150+ distribution centres (DCs) in the US, each serving 75-100 stores within a 300-mile radius. The cross-docking system means most merchandise spends less than 24 hours in a DC. Walmart's private truck fleet (10,000+ trucks) provides end-to-end visibility from supplier factory to store shelf. The Retail Link supplier portal gives Walmart's 100,000+ suppliers real-time access to their product's sales velocity at every store, enabling suppliers to manage replenishment proactively.

Walmart's Everyday Low Cost (EDLC) strategy means relentlessly eliminating costs from every part of the business — store design (functional rather than fancy), staffing levels, supplier terms (Walmart's legendary tough negotiating is famous), advertising spend, and real estate. EDLC enables EDLP (Everyday Low Price) — Walmart doesn't run weekly specials like traditional grocers because the everyday price is already low. This simplifies operations, reduces shopper confusion, and builds loyalty through price predictability.

Walmart's omnichannel strategy leverages its physical footprint as a fulfilment advantage over pure-play e-commerce. 90% of Americans live within 10 miles of a Walmart store — this proximity enables same-day delivery for e-commerce orders fulfilled from store inventory, click-and-collect curbside pickup, and same-day grocery delivery faster than Amazon Fresh can deliver from its own warehouses. The store-as-fulfilment-centre model is Walmart's structural e-commerce advantage that Amazon cannot easily replicate.

💰How Walmart Makes Money

Walmart's revenue comes primarily from retail product sales, but advertising, financial services, and Walmart+ are rapidly growing higher-margin revenue streams.

Retail merchandise accounts for the vast majority of Walmart's $650B+ revenue. Grocery (30%+ of US Walmart revenue), General Merchandise (electronics, apparel, toys), and Health and Wellness (pharmacy, optical) are the main categories. Walmart's gross margin is modest (~25% overall) because EDLP passes cost savings to customers. Operating efficiency — inventory turns, shrinkage control, labour productivity — determines profitability at these thin margins.

Walmart Connect (advertising) is the highest-growth revenue segment. With 240M weekly customers and first-party purchase data, Walmart can offer Consumer Packaged Goods (CPG) companies targeted advertising at the point of purchase — inside Walmart's own ecosystem. Walmart Connect grew 30%+ year-over-year and is approaching $3B+ annually. This advertising margin (CPG brands pay to promote their products on Walmart.com and in-store) dramatically exceeds Walmart's retail margins.

Walmart+ subscription ($12.95/month or $98/year) includes free delivery on orders over $35, fuel discounts at Walmart/Murphy gas stations, and Paramount+ streaming. With 30M+ subscribers, Walmart+ generates $3.5B+ annually in subscription revenue while creating a loyal shopping frequency increase in subscribers. Walmart Financial Services (check cashing, MoneyGram transfers, Walmart Pay) provides financial services to under-banked customers, particularly valuable in Walmart's rural store footprint.

📅History & Timeline

From a discount store in Rogers, Arkansas to the world's largest company.

1962 — First Walmart Store Opens

Sam Walton opens the first Walmart Discount Store in Rogers, Arkansas. The Everyday Low Price concept is born.

1972 — NYSE Listing

Walmart goes public on NYSE. Early investors who held the stock would see extraordinary returns over the following decades.

1992 — Becomes #1 US Retailer

Walmart surpasses Sears and Kmart to become the largest retailer in the United States.

2000 — Walmart.com Launches

E-commerce site launches. The first attempt to translate EDLP to the internet — initial execution is slow and limited.

2016 — $3.3B Jet.com Acquisition

Acquires Jet.com and its CEO Marc Lore, bringing digital-native leadership and a commitment to serious e-commerce investment.

2020 — Walmart+ Launches

Walmart+ subscription service launches as a direct Amazon Prime competitor — free delivery, fuel discounts, Paramount+ streaming.
